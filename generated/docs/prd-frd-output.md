# PRD/FRD Output

## Purpose

This stage produces the final business-facing or functional artifact that describes the delivered change in a durable, reviewable format.

## When To Use PRD vs FRD

- Use a **PRD** when the output is primarily product-oriented, stakeholder-facing, and focused on goals, user value, and measurable outcomes.
- Use an **FRD** when the output is primarily functional, engineering-facing, and focused on behavior, interfaces, rules, and validation expectations.

## Recommended PRD Structure

1. Overview
2. Business Problem
3. Goals and Success Metrics
4. Users and Roles
5. Scope
6. Non-Goals
7. Constraints
8. Delivery Summary
9. Risks and Follow-Up

## Recommended FRD Structure

1. Overview
2. Functional Scope
3. Actors and Permissions
4. Functional Requirements
5. Non-Functional Constraints
6. Interfaces and Affected Components
7. Validation Strategy
8. Open Risks or Deferred Work

## Required Inputs

The final PRD or FRD should draw from:

- gathered requirement records
- clarified requirement statements
- accepted ADRs
- delivered implementation summary
- test evidence
- change preparation notes

## AI Assistance

AI can help by:

- consolidating artifacts into a coherent final document
- normalizing terminology
- highlighting requirement coverage and gaps
- producing executive and engineering summaries from the same evidence set

## Exit Criteria

PRD/FRD output is complete when:

- the final document reflects what was actually delivered
- major decisions and constraints are visible
- validation evidence is represented
- the artifact is understandable to its intended audience
